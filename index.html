The Accreditation Proccess: A brief overview and its significance

Accreditation is a form of evaluation. It is a process of looking into the strengths, and weaknesses as well as areas which are needing improvement of programs and institutions in certain parameters determined or established by the accrediting body, normally a third party or an external party. It is a means of ensuring that programs and administration of these programs are within acceptable standards within the level they are being accredited. The ultimate end of accreditation is quality assurance.

Accreditation is important for a number of reasons. It provides assurance to the public that an organization program or institution has met established standards of quality, and that it has undergone a rigorous evaluation process by an independent accrediting agency. Accreditation can also help organizations to improve their operations and outcomes by providing them with feedback and guidance on areas where they need to improve.

For educational institutions, accreditation is particularly important because it can impact a student’s ability to transfer credits to other institutions or to obtain professional licensure. Many employers also look for job candidates who have graduated from accredited institutions as it is seen as a mark of quality and rigor.

In the Philippines, state and even local colleges and universities are submitting their curricular programs and course offering to AACCUP for accreditation. Normally, AACCUP evaluates 10 areas (10 Standards/ Key Result Areas) in all programs it evaluates. These areas are :

  • Area I : Vision, Mission, Goals, and Objectives
  • Area II : Faculty
  • Area III : Curriculum and Instruction
  • Area IV: Support to Students
  • Area V : Research
  • Area VI: Extension and Community Involvement Area
  • VII: Library Area
  • VIII: Physical Plant and Facilities
  • Area IX: Laboratories
  • Area X : Administration

A certain degree program being submitted for accreditation has to obtain the desired over-all mean of these areas to be granted or awarded the level of accreditation it is applying for. As the level advances, so is the over-all mean needed to obtain the accreditation. That is to say, Level 1 needs a lower over-all mean rating compared to Level 2, and so on.

Every area has parameters with benchmarks/standards that need to be satisfied. For example, Area 3 – Curriculum and Instruction, has six parameters and each parameter has indicators which the accreditor concerned has to rate (Item Rating) from NA (Not Applicable) to 5 (Excellent) and solve for the System – Implementation-Outcome (SIOM) Mean and eventually, for the Parameter Mean (PM) based on physical and triangulated evidences and documents presented.

To illustrate, Area 3 Parameter A has 3 System Inputs and Processes. System 1 has 3 benchmarks or indicators. So, each indicator should be rated by the accreditor with either NA, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 or 5 as the case may be and placed at the end of each indicator in the accreditation instrument. Then the means of the 3 indicators should be reflected in the SIOM column in the same instrument. When all the indicators – each in the System Inputs and Processes, Implementation and Outcome/s – have been rated, the Parameter Mean (M) shall now be computed by adding the 3 means and dividing the sum by three. The result will be the rating of the program in Parameter A of Area 3.

VISSION OF AACCUP

The long-range vision of AACCUP is to develop among SUCs a “culture of quality” such that quality assurance is then rested inside the school system, that SUCs shall maintain a certified roster of quality programs and processes.

MISSION OF AACCUP

To make the attainment of quality in education an integral part of the higher education systems more particularly among chartered state colleges and universities in the Philippines, through a sustained program of internal and external assessment. Under its charter, one of the functions, if not the main purpose of AACCUP, is "to develop a mechanism of, and conduct the evaluation of programs and institutions."

GOALS OF AACCUP

The AACCUP aims to :

  • Develop a mechanism for, and conduct the evaluation of programs and institutions for accreditation;
  • Develop a mechanism for, and conduct the evaluation of programs and institutions for accreditation;
  • establish policies and develop standards and instruments for evaluating programs/institutions;
  • generate public understanding of, earn acceptance and adapt the professional practice of accreditation to the Philippine setting; and
  • perform other activities that may directly or indirectly lead to the attainment of the purposes of accreditation.

The rationale for Conducting Accreditation

Importance of accreditation in ensuring quality education

Accreditation aims for and promotes quality education. It is very important if not indispensable to the whole educational system particularly in ensuring that program offerings are at par with standards set by authorities and accrediting bodies. When program offerings and institutions are accredited, the ones who most benefit are the major clients of the educational institutions who are the students.

Consequently, parents become confident that their children are in good hands and that they get the worth of their money. Even if we say that public education in the Philippines is free across all levels, accreditation is a means to determine that the taxpayers’ money is well spent. After all, quality education is the thrust of all stakeholders of an educational institution.

That is why, even in accreditation processes, there is an involvement or presence of students, alumni, community and local government officials and whose views and ideas become sources of information if not strong bases for triangulation of claims the institution or the program being accredited are establishing.

Explanation of how accreditation enhances program design and evaluation

Accreditation enhances program design and evaluation since it is the very core of this process. It establishes the credibility of offering programs by higher education institutions. The meticulous process of accreditation spells out the very essence of evaluation to ensure its ultimate end which is quality education.

Accreditation ensures also that as degree offering advances in terms of number of years which it is being offered, the quality of its systems and inputs, its implementation as well as its outputs also advances accordingly.

It also promotes quality of program offerings by setting benchmarks amoung institutions with lower-level accreditations with those who have already advanced in the field of accreditation.

HISTORY OF ISUFST

On June 22, 1957, Congressman Ricardo Y. Ladrido authored and sponsored Republic Act 1925, converting the Barotac Nuevo High School (BNHS) into a national school of fisheries known as Central Iloilo National School of Fisheries (CINSOF), with Mr. Amedeo S. Timbol as its first principal. In 1961, the late Don Juan Paranpan donated a lot in Tiwi, Barotac Nuevo, Iloilo, and broadened the school’s scope of curricular offerings to include collegiate courses. The passage of R.A. 321 converted the Iloilo National School of Fisheries into the Iloilo Regional School of Fisheries. In 1971, Bachelor of Science in Fishery Education (BSFED), with Dr. Elpidio M. Icamina as superintendent, was offered. In 1975, Bachelor of Science in Fisheries, with majors in Marine Fisheries, Inland Fisheries, and Fish Processing Technology, was offered.

In 1976, the school broadened its offerings with courses such as: Associate in Agriculture; Bachelor of Science in Agriculture, majors in Animal Husbandry and Agronomy; Bachelor of Science in Marine Transportation; and Bachelor of Science in Practical Arts. Another milestone in the history of the school was the offering of Master of Arts in Teaching Vocational Education, in consortium with the Iloilo School of Arts and Trades, Iloilo City. Because of P.D. 1523 signed June 11, 1978, the Iloilo Regional School of Fisheries was converted into the Iloilo State College of Fisheries on August 21, 1978, through the efforts of Dr. Narciso D. Monfort, the leadership of Dr. Elpidio M. Icamina as its first president, and the cooperation of the faculty and staff of the school and the good people of Barotac Nuevo. This marked the birth of the first and only state college of fisheries in the country.

Iloilo State College of Fisheries offered excellent training in the field of fisheries and advanced education both on campus and other satellite schools. The state college broadened its curricular offerings in Technology, Educational Management, and Public Administration; a postgraduate degree in Rural Development; Bachelor of Science in Marine Biology; Bachelor of Science in Fisheries; Bachelor of Science in Marine Transportation; Bachelor in Secondary Education; and Bachelor of Science in Ecological Tourism.

In 1979, the Marine Science Research Center was constructed in Lapuz-Lapuz, Dumangas, Iloilo. On March 3, 1983, the Old Sagay Barangay High School in Sagay, Negros Occidental, was converted into ISCOF—Sagay Branch. In 1984, the BSMT building was constructed in Alacaygan, Banate, Iloilo. On Dr. Icamina’s retirement, Mr. Benigno P. Panistante was installed as second ISCOF president on July 1, 1987.

In the 80’s, other programs were approved: Fisheries Management as one of the major fields of BS Fisheries (BSF); a ladderized curriculum of the Bachelor of Science in Marine Transportation (BSMT); and Certificate in Marine Transportation (CMT). The State College of Agriculture ASCA-ISCOF graduate education consortium program on Master of Arts in Educational Management (MAEM), major in Vocational Productivity, was also started

In 1992, Dr. Elpidio A. Locsin, Jr. was elected as the third president. Major developments were implemented in the College.

In consonance with RA 8760 in 2002, the following CHED-Supervised Institutions (CSIs) were integrated with ISCOF as its Branch Schools: Dumangas Polytechnic College (DPC), Dumangas, Iloilo; Barotac Nuevo Polytechnic Institute (BNPI), Barotac Nuevo, Iloilo; San Enrique Polytechnic College (SEPC), San Enrique, Iloilo; and Dingle Agricultural and Technical College (DATEC), Dingle, Iloilo. Dr. Wenceslao O. Sison, PASUC President, was designated OIC President in view of the vacancy left by Dr. Locsin.

In 2007, the fifth president, Dr. Ramon C. Cabag, was sworn into office. Due to his secondment, he returned to his original school, the West Visayas State University, after two years. The Regional Director of the Commission on Higher Education acted as the OIC President for a year as a result of the vacancy left by Dr. Cabag.

On March 16, 2008, the Board of Trustees installed into office the sixth president, Dr. Ma. Rosario Allones Panes. Dr. Panes started working for the university-hood of the College, with the strong support of the Honorable Congressman Ferjenel G. Biron who sponsored the bill.

The glimpses of the real thing

Explanation of the roles and responsibilities of the accreditation team members

The Accreditation Team Organization :

  1. The Over-all Coordinator. This is the case when there are 3-4 programs being accredited in one institution. Under the Over-all Coordinator are the :
    • Team Leaders and the
    • Lead Accreditors/Members
  2. The Over-all Coordinator (OC) : Assumes Over-all Leadership
    • Coordinates the activities of the team
    • Presidess over joint meetings
    • Serve as liason working with his/her counterpart in the host institution
    • Gives orientation on accreditation process
    • Acts as Spokeperson during the exit conference
    • Serves as back up accreditor in the different areas.
  3. The Team Leader
    • Assumes leadership of the survey team in a program under review
    • Collaborates with the OC in supervising the activities of the team
    • Calls and presides over team meetings
    • Collects the filled up accreditation instrument at the end of the online visit
    • Assists the members of the team
    • Consolidates and rewrites the final team report and submits it to OC
  4. The Lead Accreditors/Members
    • Work on their area of assignment and cooperates in making a well prepared report
    • Be familiar with contents of the self-survey instrument, compliance report, PPP and survey instruments
    • Make evaluation based on the survey instrument and rating system
    • Be good team player
    • Prepare a well-analyzed and written summary of findings and recommendations
    • Always act professionally

Insights and challenges encountered during the accreditation process

Accreditation as an activity or process is not new to me, as I mentioned above. I have helped during the accreditations of several programs in the university campus I am serving. So practically, the activity we conducted at ISUFST Main Campus was generally smooth sailing for me personally.

However, it is inevitable that there are some minor challenges that I encountered during the activity day. Firstly, the distance from my place. The site we visited was quite far from our place and since I have no private car or service vehicle, I had to commute and had to ask a classmate who has a car to wait for me in Zarraga, Iloilo so I can hitch ride with him along with our professor and two of my classmates. Since travelling by bus is not predictable, I came late in our rendezvous. I was ashamed to let them wait for me. I hoped they did not wait for me for a long time.

Secondly, travelling for me is always a challenge since I take maintenance medications for my hypertension and diabetes. We arrived at the site on time but I had to eat my lunch first in their campus cafeteria for me to take my meds. As a consequence, I started my tasks quite a little late than the rest of the group.

Thirdly, the documents given to us for evaluation were scanned copies the University submitted to AACCUP last year when they applied for Level 4 status of their BSED program with all the field of specializations under it. The fact that the AACCUP itself has closed the system, it was a real challenge for us to verify the claims especially that most of the claims or documents were supplemented by supporting documents that were hyperlinked but since the whole sets of documents were in PDF form, there was no way for us to click and open those links. Triangulation then was a definite challenge.

Finally, although we only looked into Area III - Curriculum and Instruction for only one specialization (I was assigned to BSED - English group), the time given to us to check the documents against the benchmarks in the six parameters of Area III was too short for us to have done much. It would have been more enriching if time would not have been limited. If we were to count, our travel time to and from that place was just equal or even longer that the time we spent for the actual doing of the thing.

Lessons learned and personal growth from conducting the Accreditation

Generally, the activity was very enriching, fruitful and productyive. There were many things I personally learned from the whole process starting from the planning stage until the culmination of the accreditation day. For one, accreditation is a teamwork. Cooperation and participation of everyone in the Team are significant for the attainment of the objectives of the activity.

Another is that, studying about the things to be done and how to do them is also very important. There is no better substitute than to go to the battle armed and ready.

Next, evaluation is a process of being reasonable, just and fair. Making judgments should never rely on what are being presented per se. Triangulation is a must. If an accreditor thinks that the documents presented are lacking, he/she should find all means to verify the claims. He/she should always check and go over his /her ratings given and compromise or adjust if needed.

Finally, the activity refreshed and enhanced the training I attended with ACCUP on Outcomes-based Accreditation. Though I have not been called by AACCUP to serve as accreditor so far, yet I completed a short term training course on it. The activity is a welcoming prelude to the Level 4 accreditation that we are preparing for come August or September.

Do's and Don'ts of Writing Reports

Writing the Strengths

Strengths hilght the areas or situations where program excels.
Strengths identify areas or situations which reflect Best Practices.
Strengths identify areas or situations which serve as a Model.

Examples:

  1. The DevCom program has been identified as Center of Excellence by the CHED.
  2. library hours are extended up to 9:00 p.m. on Sundays and Saturdays.
  3. Poor but devserving students are accomodated in the Univeristy Dormitory for free in exchange of a four-hour week service rendered as dormitory assistants....

Writing Areas Needing Improvement (ANI)

Areas Needing Improvement (ANI) identifies a specific problem.
Areas Needing Improvement (ANI) identifies a specific problem.

Writing the ANI

Examples:

  1. There is no Extention Plan which can guide the faculty members in implementing extension program/projects/actives
  2. The absense of a covered walk makes it difficult for subjects to tansfer from one building to another when it rains.

Writing Recommentations

  • Recommendations are either mandatory or for enhancement.
  • Mandatory recommendations are for compliance before the award of Levels 3 and 4; while recommendations for enhancement are for compiance after the award of Levels 1, 2, 3, and 4.
  • Recommendations for enhancement should be written in a suggestive, and NOT in a prescriptive manner.
  • Recommendations are premised by the ANI. There should be a one-toone correspondence between the ANI and the recommendations (especially for Levels 1 and 2).
Example 1: Observation (ANI)

There is no Extention Plan, which can guide the faculty members in implementing and monitoring extension programs, projects, and activities (PPAs).

Recommendation
FAculty members are best guided by an Extension Plan in implementing and monitoring extension programs, projects, and activities.

Example 2: Observation (ANI)

Covered walk is absent in the school premisses. The absence makes it difficult to transfer from one building to another when it rains.

Recommendation
The Administration might consider the construction of covered walks in between buildings on campus.

Example 3: Observation (ANI)

Research outputs of the faculty are not published in peer-reviewed journals, which are indexed by Scopus, ISI, Clarivate Analytics, Asian Citiation, and other reputable indexing bodies..

Recommendation
To boost research productivity, it would be desirable if the University encourages the faculty researchesr to publish their research outputs in peer-reviewed journals, which are indexed by Scopus or other reputable indexing bodies. Publication support and incentives would be very helpful in achieving research productivity.

Writing the Final Report

  • Findings as bases for recommendations should be document-based and validated through interviews, and other nesessary procedures/measures.
  • The Narrative Profile does not cover all claims. As much as possible we have to use triangulation for a verified, valid and reliable report.
  • The Narrative Profile may exaggerate or understate claims. Some claims are well supported, in some cases, you need to look for it.
  • The absence of evidence is not necesarrily the evidence of absence.
  • Never presume anything. Look for hard pieces of evidence.
  • Do not be taken in by glossy brochures. Marketing and promotions materials are different form reality.
  • Findings should be congruent with the ratings.
  • Approach the writing in a factual, analytical, and reflective manner to achieve a reasonable feedback report.
  • Refrain from giving opinions, perceptions, prescriptions, and motherhood statements. Substantiate very statement made.
  • Demonstrate objective professional judgment, free from bias or preconceived notions, thoroughly validated, and verified.
  • Use clear, simple, and easily comprehensible language.
  • Reflect rudiments of grammar, syntax, and logic
  • Avoid jargons (special term or expression of a particular group or field).
  • Ideas should not conflict with each other.
  • State facts straight to the point.
  • Avoid verbosity or verbiage (the use of too many words to express an idea).
  • No beating around the bush.
  • Avoid derogatory remkarks or disparaging words.
  • Avoid unreasonable recommendations.
  • In case the score initially given by the area lead accreditor is changed during the team deliberation, the area lead accreditor should ensure that the comments and suggestions of the other accreditors are incorporated in the feedback report to reflect the new score.
  • Proff reading and editing are necessary.
Common errors in writing the final report
Writing sweeping generalizations.
Lifting statements verbatim from the benchmark statements making it as strengths.
Lacking of congruence between observations (ANI) and recommendations.
Overuse of words: wanting, limited, revisit.
Repetitions use of phrases: if funds, warrant, it is recommendd.
Erroneous subject-verb agreement..
Inappropriate or wrong use or words.
Misspelled words: accommodate, recommend, assimilate, occasion, satellite, liaison, etc.
Errors in parallelism.
Recommendations are stated only in words or phrases.
Abridged report.
Use of many superlatives to acknowledge people, especially school officials.
Copy-paste reporting.

Preparing the Team Leader's Report

Fill out the forms properly provided for the purpose. Do not leave any item unanswered. Put NA for items that are not applicable.
Write in short, simple, declarative sentences. KISS - Keep it short and simple!
